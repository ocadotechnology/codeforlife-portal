A guide to using Rapid Router at home!

Rapid Router is a fun, free-forever educational game which is used in over XXX schools around the world to help children agend 6-15 learn to program.

We have been providing free lessons plans, printable worksheets and supportive videos to teachers since 2014. Not all of these teachers are programming experts, so we have designed our teaching resources with that in mind.

These resources are usually only available to educational institutions, but during this unusual time, we thought that parents may find them useful too! They are all aligned to the National Curriculum.

Learning to program is a very important skill, it teaches problem-solving, logical thinking and encourages resilience and planning. There are many careers that use programming as a skill, and it will equip your child for the digital future that awaits them.

You are very welcome to access the lesson plans that teachers can during this lockdown period, but we realise that you may have very little time to plan in detail, so we have also provided a short parents’ guide to Rapid Router.

We hope you find these resources helpful and that you and your children enjoy learning from them.

Resources for Parents

How to support your child

If you have time to support in using Rapid Router, you can make use of the teaching resources to guide them and expand their learning. However, if you are too busy to do this, you should find that your child is happy to work unaided once you have got them started. If you need help with any level, or have a general question about the game, please contact us and we will help you as quickly as we can.

You can see your child’s progress in the scoreboard. Our teaching resources are split into UK school key stages.

What will my child learn about?

By planning routes, controlling a van using movement commands and working out the shortest route to destination, your child will be learning about algorithms and how they relate to programs.

They will also learn how to create and debug simple programs and use logical reasoning to predict the behaviour of simple programs. These are all part of the Computing strand of the English National Curriculum.
